How Our Brain Is Controlling Us

Thoughts on the subconscious, dopamine loops, and the student mind.

Dec 19, 2024

4 min read

We like to believe we are the pilots of our own lives. We sit in the cockpit, hands on the wheel, convinced that every decision—from what to eat to when to study—is a conscious choice. But the neuroscience suggests otherwise. We are merely passengers in a vehicle driven by a silent, autopilot system: the subconscious mind.

The College Illusion

College life is perhaps the most critical battleground for this internal war. It is an environment designed to exploit the brain’s reward circuitry. The subconscious craves efficiency and pleasure. It builds habits to save energy. When you reach for your phone the moment you wake up, that isn't you deciding. It’s a dopamine loop cemented by repetition. You are fighting an algorithm that knows you better than you know yourself.

The Dopamine Trap

Social validation, infinite scroll, the "next episode" button—these are not random distractions. They are inputs that hijack the brain's primitive desire for novelty. As a student or a builder, your ability to do deep work is your only currency. Yet, we trade this currency for cheap, short-term hits of chemical satisfaction. The brain prioritizes the "now" over the "later" because survival used to depend on it. But in the modern world, success depends on the exact opposite: delayed gratification.

Rewiring the System

So, how do we take back the controls? Awareness is the first step. You must observe the urge without acting on it. When the brain screams for a distraction, recognize it as a biological signal, not a command. Identity is the second step. Stop saying "I'm trying to focus" and start saying "I am a person who focuses." The subconscious aligns actions with identity.

We build systems to scale code and trade. It is time we build systems to scale our own minds.

Let's build the future.
